# Risk Assessment & Verdict
### Honest failure probability, stage-by-stage failure modes, critical success factors, go/no-go, and kill criteria
**Prepared 2026-06-26 · Design-led desk/workspace gear brand · Toronto founder**

> This is the brutally-honest section the founder asked for. No motivational filler. Where the data argues against the plan, it says so.

---

## 1. Honest probability of failure

**Base-rate reality:** roughly **20% of new businesses fail in year one and ~50% within five years** (long-run US/Canada small-business norms). Consumer-product DTC brands skew worse early because they tie up cash in inventory before knowing if anything sells. The founder also carries a **specific scar**: a prior commodity FBA business whose account was permanently terminated.

**My estimate for *this* venture, executed as specified:**
- **Probability it fails to become a self-sustaining business (returns < capital, winds down within ~2 years): ~55–65%.**
- **Probability it becomes a modest, profitable, phone-run brand (~$250k–$1M revenue, the founder's livelihood): ~25–30%.**
- **Probability it reaches genuine Grovemade/Orbitkey scale ($10M+): ~5–10%**, and only on a 7–15 year horizon.

These are sobering but **better than the founder's last venture**, for three concrete reasons: (1) the unit economics are robust and stress-test-proof (Report 03); (2) the recommended **Kickstarter launch removes the #1 failure mode** — over-ordering inventory — by pre-selling it; (3) capital at risk is modest (CAD $40k) and preserved in base/conservative scenarios. **The wager is almost entirely on demand and differentiation, not on the economics or the capital structure.** That is the right risk to be taking.

## 3. Top reasons businesses like this fail (and the specific antidote here)
1. **They build a commodity and compete on price.** → Antidote: design system + cable-integration moat + premium materials; never the cheapest.
2. **They over-order inventory before proof.** → Antidote: Kickstarter pre-sell; strict reorder discipline.
3. **They can't acquire customers profitably.** → Antidote: content-first, seed-first, Walmart intent traffic; paid only on proven creative.
4. **They underestimate cross-border tax/logistics.** → Antidote: the de-minimis-aware operating model + professional setup (Report 04).
5. **They quit too early or, worse, pour money into a dead idea too long.** → Antidote: explicit kill criteria and reinvestment triggers (below).

---

## 4. Critical success factors (what the founder MUST execute)
1. **A genuinely differentiated, well-made hero** that passes the acceptance gate and *shows* its difference in a 5-second clip.
2. **A successful validation event** (Kickstarter funds, or a converting soft-launch) *before* committing the budget.
3. **Content discipline** — consistent, on-brand short-form + creator seeding, sustained for months, on the brand account (not the personal platform, until Year 2).
4. **Capital discipline** — no over-ordering; reorder on real sell-through; keep ≥3 months runway.
5. **Professional cross-border setup** — CPA + customs broker engaged before US import; de-minimis-aware logistics.
6. **Patience + honesty** — treat the kill criteria and reinvestment triggers as real, not aspirational.

---

## 5. Kill criteria (when to cut losses)
Cut losses / stop reinvesting if **any** of these occurs:
- **Samples fail the physical acceptance gate** and can't be fixed within budget → kill before any PO.
- **Kickstarter funds < ~50% of a realistic goal** (or a soft-launch converts below ~1% with sound traffic) → demand isn't there at a premium; do not bulk-order. Pivot the hero or stop.
- **6 months post-launch with no organic traction** (no breakout content, flat Walmart rank, reviews not accumulating) and **blended CAC stuck above ~$25** → the model can't carry paid acquisition; wind down.
- **Year 1 tracking to the downside scenario** (Report 03: ~2,000 units, ~breakeven) at month 8 → **do not reinvest**; recover inventory, decide pivot vs. orderly exit.
- **Two consecutive months of negative contribution** after fees/fulfillment/returns → structural problem; freeze spend and diagnose before continuing.

## 6. Reinvestment triggers (when to push)
Reinvest cashflow into the next SKU / more paid spend only when **all** hold:
- **3 consecutive months contribution-positive**, and
- **blended ROAS ≥ 3×** on paid (or organic carrying acquisition at < $25 CAC), and
- **reviews ≥ 4.3★ and accumulating**, and
- **≥3 months cash runway** after the reinvestment.

## 7. The verdict

**GO — conditionally, and only as specified.**

This is a **qualified go**, not an enthusiastic one. The honest odds of building a meaningful business are below 50/50, and the path to "Grovemade scale" is a 7–15 year, single-digit-percent outcome. But the structure of *this* plan makes it a **smart, capital-preserving bet**:
- The unit economics are sound and survive a simultaneous −10% price / +10% cost shock.
- The capital at risk (CAD $40k, $30k floor) is modest and preserved unless the idea is actively working.
- The Kickstarter on-ramp converts the largest risk (inventory) into a pre-sale, directly addressing the prior failure.
- The market gap is real, sourced, and currently under-occupied (with one funded threat, Orbitkey Grid, to flank not fight).

**It succeeds if:** the founder executes content + capital discipline, the hero is genuinely differentiated and validates with real money before the budget is committed, and acquisition stays cheap (content + Walmart intent). **It fails if:** the product is a prettier commodity, the founder over-orders or chases paid CAC the margin can't bear, or validation is weak but the founder presses on anyway.

**The single most important instruction:** treat the **validation gate** as binary. If real money doesn't show up before you order bulk inventory, the answer is no — and that "no" costs almost nothing. That is the entire difference between this venture and the last one.
