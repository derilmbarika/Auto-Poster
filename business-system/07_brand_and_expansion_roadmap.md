# Brand & Expansion Roadmap
### How one hero becomes a coordinated, acquirable desk-gear label
**Prepared 2026-06-26 · Design-led desk/workspace gear brand · Toronto founder**

> The founder's stated ambition: start small, but grow to Grovemade/Orbitkey scale and potentially sell. This roadmap shows the SKU sequence, the timing, how it's funded, and what makes the eventual brand actually worth buying.

---

## 1. The core idea: a system, not a catalog
The brand is **one material story + one color story** applied across an expanding set of desk objects, all sharing a **magnetic modular interface** so every piece visibly belongs to the same desk. That coherence is the moat — it is exactly what every competitor lacks (Report 01). The roadmap below adds SKUs **only** in that shared language; a SKU that doesn't fit the system doesn't ship.

**Design system (from Report 02):** one primary metal (anodized aluminum or powder-coated steel) + wool felt on every contact surface; a tight 3-neutral palette distinct from Orbitkey's; quiet, logo-light, photographs-on-any-desk forms; a shared magnetic dock.

---

## 2. SKU sequencing (each funded by the last)

| Phase | SKU | Role | Funding | Why now |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Y1** | **Modular organizer + integrated cable system** (hero) | Beachhead; the system's spine | Kickstarter pre-sale | Leads with the category that commands a premium and crowdfunds; wins on the cable void |
| **Y2** | **Standalone weighted cable anchor** + **under-desk raceway** | Low-entry-price search capture (Walmart) + attach-rate driver | Cashflow | Monetizes the differentiating feature as its own hero; cheap to ship |
| **Y2** | **Pen/EDC module + catch-all tray** | Raises AOV; deepens the "system" feel | Cashflow | Flanks Orbitkey Grid on material + cable integration |
| **Y3** | **Monitor riser / laptop stand** with integrated cable routing | Higher AOV; new search demand (monitor/laptop stand volume is large) | Cashflow + 2nd Kickstarter | Bigger object, bigger margin; routes cables into the system |
| **Y3** | **Premium desk mat** with magnetic edge that docks the cable anchors | **Halo / platform** SKU — ties the whole range together | Cashflow | Arrives last *on purpose*: launches into an existing audience as a centerpiece, not a crowded first bet |
| **Y4–5** | **Headphone stand, phone dock, shelf, colorway drops, collabs** | Range completion + brand-moment cadence | Cashflow | A recognizable, complete coordinated label |

**Timing principle:** add ~2–4 SKUs/year, each in the shared system, each validated (small KS or pre-order for the bigger ones; direct launch for cheap add-ons). Attach rate and AOV are the metrics that prove it's a *system*.

## 3. The personal-platform bridge (Year 2+, never before)
The founder's existing tech-content platform is deliberately **off the table until the product is proven**. In Year 2, once there are reviews, a look, and a real product, it becomes an **organic accelerant**: authentic "here's what I've been quietly building" storytelling, behind-the-brand content, and a warm audience to seed launches — *not* a hard-sell channel and *not* something early-stage plans depend on. Using it earlier would tie a fragile new brand to a personal identity before the brand can stand on its own; using it in Year 2 compounds proven momentum. This is a one-way accelerant, used once the flywheel is already turning.

## 4. What a "big, acquirable brand" looks like — and what drives sale value

### The benchmark
Grovemade (~$10–16M revenue, vertically integrated) and Orbitkey (~$24M, repeat-crowdfunder) are the realistic ceilings — both built over 10–15 years, both VC-free (Report 01 §1.4). "Big and acquirable" here means a **~$5–25M revenue, profitable, recognizable design label**, not a unicorn.

### What buyers actually pay for (the value drivers)
A small consumer brand typically sells for a **multiple of profit (SDE/EBITDA, ~2.5–5×) or of revenue (~1–3× for strong DTC brands)** — but the *multiple* is set by how transferable and defensible the business is. Maximize these:
1. **Brand equity & recognition** — a named, coordinated look people recognize on a desk-tour; trademarks owned.
2. **Owned audience & data** — email/SMS list, social following, repeat-customer base, LTV data. (The asset a buyer can't replicate.)
3. **Defensible reviews/ratings** — thousands of reviews at ≥4.3★ across channels = durable conversion.
4. **Diversified, stable channels** — own store + Walmart + wholesale + international; not single-platform-dependent.
5. **Clean, documented operations** — SOPs, a phone-runnable semi-automated system, so the business isn't the founder. (Report 05 is literally this asset.)
6. **Transferable supply chain** — documented suppliers, a second source, no single-factory dependency.
7. **Recurring/expanding revenue** — a system with attach rate and repeat purchase beats a one-hit SKU.
8. **Healthy margins & growth** — the 40%+ contribution and a credible growth story justify the top of the multiple range.

### What destroys value (avoid)
Single-channel dependence, founder-dependence (no SOPs), thin/unprotected brand, one-SKU concentration, messy books, China-single-source tariff exposure, fake/incentivized reviews. The roadmap above is designed to build the value drivers and avoid the value destroyers from day one.

---

## 5. The throughline
**One well-made, differentiated hero → a coordinated multi-SKU system in one design language → a recognizable, semi-automated, multi-channel brand with an owned audience → an optional, well-prepared exit.** Each step funds the next from cashflow or pre-sales; the personal platform joins only once the brand can stand alone; and every SKU and SOP is chosen to build the things an acquirer pays a premium for. The ambition is legitimate and the path is real — it is a decade-long compounding game, executed with discipline, not a sprint.
